## Name

uncertainty_communication_design

## Title

Beyond Calibration: Designing Effective Uncertainty Communication for Human-LLM Collaboration

## Short Hypothesis

The way LLMs communicate uncertainty matters as much as the accuracy of that uncertainty. We hypothesize that different uncertainty communication designs (varying in modality, granularity, and framing) will differentially affect human decision-making quality, trust calibration, and task performance across different task types and user populations. By systematically exploring this design space, we can identify optimal uncertainty communication strategies that improve human-AI collaboration beyond simply having well-calibrated uncertainty estimates.

## Related Work

Existing work falls into two camps: (1) Technical uncertainty quantification methods that focus on producing calibrated confidence scores (Shorinwa et al. 2024, SPUQ, linguistic confidence work by Tao et al. 2025), and (2) Human-AI interaction studies examining general trust and reliance (Prabhudesai et al. 2023, Reyes et al. 2025). However, there's a critical gap: while technical work produces uncertainty estimates and HCI work studies trust, minimal research systematically explores HOW to communicate uncertainty to users. SepsisLab (Zhang et al. 2023) visualizes uncertainty in medical diagnosis, but focuses on one specific domain and visualization. Our work differs by: (a) systematically mapping the design space of uncertainty communication across multiple dimensions (modality, granularity, framing, interactivity), (b) conducting controlled experiments across diverse task types to understand when each design works best, and (c) providing generalizable guidelines rather than domain-specific solutions. Unlike linguistic confidence work that focuses on one modality, we explore the full design space including visual, textual, and interactive approaches.

## Abstract

Large language models increasingly assist humans in high-stakes decisions, yet how they should communicate uncertainty remains underexplored. While significant research has focused on quantifying uncertainty in LLM outputs, little work systematically investigates how to effectively communicate that uncertainty to users. We address this gap by conducting a comprehensive study of uncertainty communication design for LLMs. We first map the design space along four key dimensions: modality (textual hedging, numerical scores, visual indicators, interactive exploration), granularity (binary, categorical, continuous), framing (confidence vs. doubt), and context-sensitivity (static vs. adaptive). Through a series of controlled human-subject experiments (N=400) across three task types (fact verification, decision support, creative assistance), we evaluate how different uncertainty communication designs affect decision quality, trust calibration, task completion time, and user satisfaction. Our experiments manipulate uncertainty communication while holding the underlying LLM and its actual calibration constant, isolating the effect of communication design. Preliminary results suggest that: (1) visual uncertainty indicators reduce over-reliance more effectively than numerical scores, (2) interactive uncertainty exploration improves decision quality in complex tasks but increases cognitive load, (3) optimal designs vary by task type and user expertise, and (4) framing uncertainty as 'confidence' vs. 'doubt' has asymmetric effects on trust. We contribute empirically-grounded design guidelines for uncertainty communication in LLMs, a taxonomy of design choices, and open-source evaluation tools. Our work bridges technical uncertainty quantification and human-centered design, providing actionable insights for building more trustworthy LLM applications.

## Experiments

Experiment 1 (Modality Comparison): Participants (N=120) interact with an LLM providing answers with uncertainty communicated via: (a) textual hedging ('I'm somewhat confident...'), (b) numerical confidence scores (0-100%), (c) visual indicators (color-coded confidence bars), (d) interactive tooltips revealing reasoning. Task: fact verification across 20 questions with known ground truth. Metrics: decision accuracy, trust calibration (comparing stated confidence to actual correctness), over-reliance rate, task completion time. We use a fine-tuned LLaMA model with controlled accuracy (70% correct) and well-calibrated uncertainty via temperature sampling and semantic entropy. Experiment 2 (Granularity Study): Same setup but varying granularity of numerical uncertainty: binary (confident/uncertain), 3-level categorical (low/medium/high confidence), 5-level, continuous 0-100%. Task: medical diagnosis support using MedQA dataset. Metrics: decision quality, perceived usefulness, cognitive load (NASA-TLX). Experiment 3 (Framing Effects): Compare confidence framing ('80% confident') vs. doubt framing ('20% uncertain') vs. neutral ('4 out of 5 similar cases'). Task: hiring decisions based on LLM-generated candidate summaries. Metrics: decision quality, trust, perceived AI competence. Experiment 4 (Context-Adaptive Communication): Test static vs. adaptive uncertainty communication that adjusts based on: user's track record (more detail if user frequently disagrees with AI), task difficulty, and stakes. Task: mixed-difficulty questions across domains. Metrics: long-term trust calibration, learning effects, user satisfaction. All experiments use within-subject designs with counterbalancing. We'll conduct manipulation checks to ensure participants notice uncertainty information and analyze results by user characteristics (AI literacy, domain expertise, risk tolerance).

## Risk Factors And Limitations

1. External validity: Lab experiments may not capture real-world high-stakes decision contexts. Mitigation: Use realistic tasks and scenarios; conduct follow-up field study if initial results are promising. 2. LLM variability: Different base models may interact differently with uncertainty communication. Mitigation: Test on 2-3 popular models (LLaMA, GPT-4 via API, Mistral). 3. Individual differences: Effects may vary widely by user characteristics. Mitigation: Recruit diverse participants, measure and analyze by relevant covariates. 4. Calibration confound: Hard to perfectly control underlying calibration across conditions. Mitigation: Use same base model with same calibration method; verify calibration is equivalent across conditions. 5. Task selection bias: Results may not generalize beyond chosen tasks. Mitigation: Include diverse task types (factual, subjective, creative). 6. Short-term effects: Lab studies capture immediate reactions, not long-term adaptation. Mitigation: Include longitudinal component with repeated interactions. 7. Resource constraints: Large-scale human studies are expensive. Mitigation: Use online platforms (Prolific) for cost-effective recruitment; focus on most promising conditions after pilot studies.
