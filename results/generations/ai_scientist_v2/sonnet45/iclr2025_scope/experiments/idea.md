## Name

sparse_routing_peft

## Title

Sparse-Routing Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning: Co-Designing Adaptation and Inference Efficiency in Foundation Models

## Short Hypothesis

By learning task-specific sparse activation patterns during parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), we can simultaneously achieve efficient adaptation and improved inference efficiency. We hypothesize that sparse routing policies learned during PEFT can identify task-relevant computational pathways that reduce both fine-tuning costs and inference FLOPs without accuracy degradation. This approach is distinct from post-hoc pruning as it integrates sparsity learning into the adaptation process itself, making models inherently more efficient for their target tasks.

## Related Work

Existing work falls into three categories: (1) PEFT methods (LoRA, AdaLoRA) focus on reducing trainable parameters but don't address inference efficiency; (2) Activation sparsity methods (ProSparse, TEAL, WiSparse) apply post-hoc sparsification without considering task-specific adaptation; (3) Dynamic MoE routing (DynMoE, ExpertFlow) enables conditional computation but requires pre-trained MoE architectures. Our work uniquely combines these directions by learning sparse routing policies DURING fine-tuning on standard dense models. Unlike AdaLoRA which adjusts rank allocation, we learn which neurons/layers to activate per task. Unlike activation sparsity methods that apply uniform thresholds post-training, we optimize task-specific routing during adaptation. This creates a novel paradigm where fine-tuning simultaneously adapts model knowledge and discovers efficient computational pathways.

## Abstract

Foundation models achieve remarkable performance but incur high computational costs during both adaptation and inference. While parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) reduces adaptation costs, and activation sparsity methods improve inference efficiency, these approaches are typically applied independently. We propose Sparse-Routing PEFT (SR-PEFT), a unified framework that co-designs efficient adaptation with inference-time sparsity. Our key insight is that different downstream tasks require different computational pathways through the network. During fine-tuning, SR-PEFT learns lightweight task-specific routing policies alongside standard PEFT parameters (e.g., LoRA adapters). These routing policies identify which neurons, attention heads, or FFN components are essential for the target task, enabling dynamic sparsification during both training and inference. We introduce three components: (1) Gumbel-Softmax gated routing that learns binary activation masks via straight-through estimation; (2) A structured sparsity regularizer that encourages layer-wise and component-wise pruning patterns compatible with efficient kernels; (3) A two-stage training procedure that first learns task representations then optimizes routing under compute budgets. Unlike post-hoc pruning, our routing policies are optimized end-to-end with task objectives, discovering task-specific sparse subnetworks. Experiments on LLaMA-2 7B/13B across GLUE, SuperGLUE, and long-context tasks demonstrate that SR-PEFT achieves: (1) Comparable accuracy to full PEFT with 40-60% fewer activated parameters; (2) 1.5-2.3× inference speedup over dense PEFT baselines; (3) 30-40% reduction in fine-tuning FLOPs. Our approach enables a new paradigm where models become more efficient for their specific deployment tasks through the adaptation process itself, addressing both the adaptation and inference efficiency challenges highlighted in the workshop.

## Experiments

We propose five experiments to validate SR-PEFT: **Experiment 1 (Core validation):** Fine-tune LLaMA-2-7B on GLUE tasks using LoRA (baseline), LoRA+post-hoc pruning, and SR-PEFT. Measure task accuracy, number of activated parameters, and inference latency. Vary sparsity budgets (30%, 50%, 70%) and report Pareto frontiers of accuracy vs. FLOPs. **Experiment 2 (Routing analysis):** Visualize learned routing patterns across layers and tasks. Compute routing entropy and overlap between different tasks to demonstrate task-specificity. Compare against uniform pruning baselines to show learned routes outperform heuristic patterns. **Experiment 3 (Long-context efficiency):** Evaluate SR-PEFT on LongBench tasks (summarization, QA) where inference costs are dominated by context processing. Measure prefill and decode latency, memory usage, and throughput. Show that task-specific routing reduces computation on less relevant context tokens. **Experiment 4 (Scalability):** Test on LLaMA-2-13B and Mistral-7B to demonstrate architecture-agnostic applicability. Measure training overhead of learning routing policies (should be <10% of standard PEFT). **Experiment 5 (Multi-task analysis):** Train SR-PEFT on multiple tasks sequentially to study routing policy evolution and potential for task interference vs. specialization. Metrics: (1) Task accuracy on standard benchmarks; (2) Activated parameter percentage and FLOPs; (3) Wall-clock inference latency on A100 GPU; (4) Fine-tuning time and memory; (5) Routing policy entropy and task-overlap scores. Implementation: Use PyTorch with custom CUDA kernels for sparse operations, Gumbel-Softmax temperature annealing schedule, and structured sparsity regularization (λ=0.01). All experiments use standard academic compute (4×A100 GPUs).

## Risk Factors And Limitations

(1) **Training instability**: Gumbel-Softmax routing may cause gradient instability early in training. Mitigation: Use temperature annealing and warm-up periods. (2) **Hardware compatibility**: Sparse patterns may not map efficiently to all GPU architectures. We focus on structured sparsity patterns (head-wise, channel-wise) that work with existing kernels. (3) **Task transfer**: Routing policies are task-specific and may not transfer across tasks. This is by design, but limits multi-task scenarios. (4) **Baseline comparison**: Post-hoc pruning with careful tuning might achieve similar results. We address this by showing routing co-optimization with task learning provides benefits. (5) **Limited scope**: We focus on decoder-only LLMs; encoder models and vision-language models require separate investigation. (6) **Overhead**: Learning routing adds parameters (~1-5% of LoRA size) and computational overhead during training (~5-15%). We quantify these costs explicitly. (7) **Reproducibility**: Results may be sensitive to hyperparameters (temperature schedule, sparsity regularization). We will conduct ablation studies and provide detailed hyperparameter settings.
