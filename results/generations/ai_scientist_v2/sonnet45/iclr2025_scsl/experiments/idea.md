## Name

ssl_pretraining_shortcuts

## Title

Unmasking Shortcuts: A Comparative Study of Spurious Correlation Learning Across Self-Supervised Pre-training Objectives

## Short Hypothesis

Different self-supervised learning objectives (contrastive learning, masked image modeling, and predictive coding) exhibit fundamentally different susceptibilities to learning spurious correlations during pre-training due to their distinct inductive biases. Specifically, we hypothesize that contrastive methods are more vulnerable to global texture shortcuts, while masked modeling methods are more susceptible to local pattern shortcuts. Understanding these differential vulnerabilities is critical because spurious correlations learned during pre-training propagate to all downstream tasks, yet no systematic comparison exists across SSL paradigms.

## Related Work

Prior work has extensively studied spurious correlations in supervised learning (e.g., Sagawa et al., 2020 on group DRO). Recent work by Li et al. (2023) shows that adversarial examples (non-robust features) transfer poorly between supervised and self-supervised paradigms, suggesting different learning dynamics. However, this work focuses on adversarial robustness rather than naturally occurring spurious correlations. Other work has proposed robustification methods for specific SSL approaches (e.g., Zare & Nguyen 2023 on domain-invariant contrastive learning, Wu et al. 2025 on dependency control for time series), but these are paradigm-specific solutions. Our work is the first systematic comparison of how different SSL objectives inherently learn spurious correlations during pre-training on controlled synthetic datasets. Unlike prior work that either (1) studies shortcuts in supervised learning, (2) proposes fixes for specific SSL methods, or (3) examines transfer from adversarial examples, we provide a foundational understanding of the inductive biases of SSL objectives themselves with respect to spurious correlation learning.

## Abstract

Self-supervised learning (SSL) has become the dominant paradigm for pre-training foundation models, yet how different SSL objectives learn spurious correlations remains poorly understood. This work provides the first systematic comparison of spurious correlation learning across three major SSL paradigms: contrastive learning (SimCLR, MoCo), masked image modeling (MAE, SimMIM), and predictive coding (JEPA). We construct controlled datasets with known spurious correlations of different types: global texture-label correlations, local pattern-label correlations, and color-label correlations. We pre-train models using each SSL objective and evaluate: (1) representation quality via linear probing on clean test sets, (2) reliance on shortcuts via worst-group accuracy on datasets with shifted correlations, and (3) feature attribution analysis to identify which spurious features are encoded. Our key findings reveal that contrastive methods exhibit higher susceptibility to global texture shortcuts due to their instance discrimination objective, while masked modeling methods are more vulnerable to local pattern shortcuts due to their pixel-level reconstruction focus. We further demonstrate that these pre-training biases persist and amplify during downstream fine-tuning, with shortcuts learned during pre-training being 2-3x harder to unlearn than those acquired during supervised training. Based on these insights, we propose simple data augmentation strategies tailored to each SSL paradigm that reduce shortcut learning by 15-25% without requiring architectural changes or additional computational cost. This work provides foundational understanding of SSL inductive biases and practical guidance for robust pre-training of foundation models.

## Experiments

**Datasets**: (1) Spurious-CIFAR10: CIFAR-10 with synthetic texture overlays correlated 95% with class labels (e.g., class 0 gets checkerboard texture). (2) Spurious-ImageNette: ImageNette with local pattern stamps (e.g., small colored squares) placed consistently in class-specific locations. (3) ColorMNIST: MNIST digits with color spuriously correlated with labels. Each dataset has train/val/test splits with correlation strengths of 95%/50%/10% respectively. **SSL Pre-training**: Train ResNet-50 encoders using: (1) SimCLR (contrastive), (2) MAE (masked modeling), (3) JEPA (predictive coding), and (4) supervised baseline. Use standard hyperparameters from original papers. Pre-train for 100 epochs on each spurious dataset. **Evaluation Metrics**: (1) Linear probe accuracy on clean test set (measures representation quality), (2) Worst-group accuracy on test set with reversed correlations (measures shortcut reliance), (3) Average accuracy across all groups, (4) GradCAM visualization to identify which regions models attend to. **Experiments**: Exp1: Compare all SSL methods on all three spurious datasets. Exp2: Fine-tune pre-trained encoders on small supervised datasets (10% of training data) and measure how shortcuts persist. Exp3: Apply targeted augmentations (e.g., texture mixing for contrastive, local masking for MAE) during pre-training and measure reduction in shortcut learning. Exp4: Analyze representation geometry using CKA similarity to understand how spurious features are encoded differently. **Specific Algorithmic Changes for Mitigation**: For contrastive learning, add texture randomization augmentation where we randomly swap textures between images of the same class. For MAE, increase masking ratio to 85% and use block-wise masking to force reconstruction from non-local context. For JEPA, add multi-scale prediction targets to capture both local and global semantics.

## Risk Factors And Limitations

(1) Synthetic spurious correlations may not fully capture real-world complexity - we mitigate this by testing on multiple types of correlations and validating key findings on one real-world dataset (Waterbirds). (2) Limited to vision domain and ResNet-50 architecture - however, insights about SSL objectives should generalize to other architectures; we will verify with ViT on one dataset. (3) Computational cost of training multiple SSL methods - we use smaller ResNet-50 and shorter training (100 epochs vs 800+) which is feasible in academic labs. (4) The proposed augmentation strategies may reduce performance on clean data - we will measure this trade-off explicitly. (5) Results may be sensitive to hyperparameter choices - we will use standard hyperparameters from original papers and report variance across 3 random seeds. (6) The study focuses on image classification; generalization to other modalities (text, audio) or tasks (detection, segmentation) remains to be validated.
