## Name

adaptive_ai_assertiveness

## Title

Context-Adaptive AI Assertiveness: Dynamically Calibrating AI Challenge Levels Based on Human Cognitive Load in Collaborative Decision-Making

## Short Hypothesis

AI systems that dynamically adjust their interaction style (supportive vs. challenging) based on real-time cognitive load indicators will achieve better decision quality, preserve human agency, and foster appropriate reliance compared to fixed-persona AI assistants. This addresses the fundamental tension between engaging users through challenge and maintaining performance under cognitive strain, enabling truly bidirectional alignment where AI adapts to human state while preserving human autonomy.

## Related Work

Recent work has explored fixed AI personas (Jin et al., 2025) showing contrarian AI improves discourse but reduces satisfaction, while supportive AI increases agreement but may reduce critical thinking. Cognitive Forcing Functions (Chen et al., 2025) improve engagement but can harm performance by increasing cognitive load. Work on 'when not to help' (Steyvers & Mayer, 2025) addresses timing of assistance but not modulation of interaction style. Dual process theory has been applied to AI agent reasoning (Zhang et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) but not to adapting how AI interacts with humans. Our work uniquely combines cognitive load detection with dynamic AI persona switching, creating a system that adjusts its assertiveness based on human state rather than maintaining fixed interaction patterns. This is not simply combining existing approaches but investigating a fundamentally new question: should AI challenge level vary with cognitive context, and how does this affect bidirectional alignment?

## Abstract

Effective human-AI collaboration requires balancing AI assistance with human agency, yet current systems use fixed interaction styles that ignore dynamic human cognitive states. Research shows contrarian AI improves critical thinking but reduces satisfaction, while supportive AI maintains comfort but may encourage overreliance. We propose context-adaptive AI assertiveness, where AI systems dynamically modulate their challenge level based on real-time cognitive load indicators. We hypothesize that adaptive systems will outperform fixed-persona approaches across multiple dimensions of bidirectional alignment. We will develop an AI decision-support system with three conditions: (1) always-supportive AI, (2) always-challenging AI, and (3) adaptive AI that switches between supportive and challenging modes based on cognitive load proxies (response latency, confidence ratings, decision revision patterns, and task complexity). Through controlled experiments in medical diagnosis and financial risk assessment tasks, we will measure decision accuracy, appropriate reliance (agreement with AI when correct, disagreement when incorrect), learning outcomes, perceived agency, and user satisfaction. We will use mixed-effects models to analyze how adaptation improves outcomes and qualitative analysis to understand user perceptions of AI behavior. Expected contributions include: (1) empirical evidence for dynamic AI assertiveness in collaborative decision-making, (2) validated cognitive load proxies for real-time adaptation, (3) design principles for bidirectional alignment systems that respect both AI capabilities and human autonomy, and (4) insights into when challenge versus support is most beneficial. This work advances bidirectional alignment by demonstrating how AI can align with human cognitive state (AI-to-human) while preserving human critical evaluation capacity (human-to-AI).

## Experiments

Experiment 1 - Medical Diagnosis Task: 120 participants (40 per condition) make sequential diagnostic decisions with AI assistance. Task complexity varies across trials (simple, moderate, complex cases). Cognitive load proxies measured: response time, confidence ratings (1-7 scale), mouse movement entropy, and decision revisions. Adaptive AI switches to supportive mode when cognitive load indicators exceed threshold (>75th percentile of individual baseline) and challenging mode otherwise. Supportive mode: AI presents recommendation with confirming evidence. Challenging mode: AI presents alternative diagnosis with contradictory evidence. Metrics: (1) Decision accuracy, (2) Appropriate reliance score (agreement rate when AI correct - agreement rate when AI incorrect), (3) Learning gain (performance improvement on AI-absent test trials), (4) Perceived agency (validated scale), (5) User satisfaction. Experiment 2 - Financial Risk Assessment: 90 participants evaluate investment scenarios. Similar three-condition design with adaptive threshold based on real-time cognitive load. Task involves evaluating risk profiles where AI may be correct or incorrect. Additional metrics: (1) Calibration (alignment between confidence and accuracy), (2) Time to decision, (3) Explanation engagement (time spent reviewing AI rationale). Experiment 3 - Threshold Sensitivity Analysis: Using Experiment 1 data, systematically vary adaptation threshold (50th, 75th, 90th percentile) to identify optimal switching points. Compare performance curves across thresholds. Analysis: Mixed-effects models with condition as fixed effect, participant as random effect. Mediation analysis to test if cognitive load moderates condition effects. Qualitative interviews (n=30) to understand user perceptions of adaptive behavior and perceived AI trustworthiness.

## Risk Factors And Limitations

1. Cognitive load proxies may not perfectly capture actual cognitive state; validation against established measures needed. 2. Adaptation threshold selection is non-trivial; may require personalization beyond population-level cutoffs. 3. Users may perceive adaptive behavior as inconsistent or unpredictable, potentially reducing trust; qualitative work essential to understand this. 4. Effects may be domain-specific; medical and financial tasks may not generalize to all decision contexts. 5. Laboratory setting may not capture real-world complexity where cognitive load sources are more varied. 6. Short-term experiments may not reveal long-term adaptation effects or learning. 7. Sample limited to educated adults; may not generalize to diverse populations. 8. Challenging AI behavior must be carefully designed to avoid appearing adversarial or undermining user confidence. 9. Ethical considerations around manipulating user experience require careful IRB review and informed consent. 10. Results may show no benefit of adaptation if fixed strategies are sufficient or if switching costs outweigh benefits.
