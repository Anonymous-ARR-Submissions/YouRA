## Name

representation_guided_data_mixing

## Title

Representation Health Monitoring for Dynamic Data Mixing in Foundation Model Pre-training

## Short Hypothesis

Foundation models exhibit detectable geometric signatures in their learned representations (e.g., attention entropy, feature space clustering quality, layer-wise representation collapse) that can serve as early indicators of data source imbalance or overfitting. By monitoring these representation health metrics during pre-training and dynamically adjusting data mixing ratios accordingly, we can achieve more balanced learning across diverse data sources without relying solely on loss or gradient signals. This approach is particularly valuable for multi-source foundation model training where different data domains may have varying learning dynamics that manifest in representation geometry before affecting downstream performance.

## Related Work

Recent work on adaptive data mixing (PiKE, GRAPE) optimizes sampling weights based on task-specific losses or gradient conflicts. However, these methods are reactive—they respond after performance issues manifest. Our approach differs fundamentally by using intrinsic representation quality metrics as proactive signals. While recent work has studied attention patterns in transformers (e.g., 'Bayesian Geometry of Transformer Attention'), no prior work has leveraged these geometric properties to guide data source selection during pre-training. Unlike curriculum learning approaches that follow predefined schedules, our method adapts based on the model's actual learned representations. This is also distinct from domain adaptation work (MDGPT, MP^2A) which focuses on aligning pre-trained models to new domains, whereas we address the pre-training phase itself with multiple concurrent data sources.

## Abstract

Foundation models are typically pre-trained on diverse data sources, but determining optimal mixing ratios remains challenging. Existing approaches rely on task-specific loss signals or gradient analysis, which are reactive and may miss early signs of learning imbalance. We propose a novel framework that monitors the geometric health of learned representations during pre-training to dynamically adjust data mixing ratios. Specifically, we track three complementary metrics: (1) attention entropy across layers to detect over-specialization to specific data sources, (2) feature space clustering quality via silhouette scores to assess representation collapse, and (3) layer-wise CKA similarity to identify premature convergence. When these metrics indicate unhealthy learning dynamics for a particular data source, we adjust its sampling probability. We validate our approach on multi-domain language model pre-training using a mixture of Wikipedia, code repositories, scientific papers, and conversational data. Our method requires minimal computational overhead (<1% training time) and provides interpretable insights into the learning process. Experiments show that representation-guided mixing achieves 3-5% improvement in downstream task performance across diverse benchmarks compared to static mixing baselines, while maintaining better balance across data sources. Importantly, our approach detects and corrects learning imbalances 2-3 epochs earlier than gradient-based methods, preventing wasted computation. This work opens a new direction for data-centric optimization in foundation model training by leveraging the rich geometric structure of learned representations.

## Experiments

1. **Baseline Setup**: Pre-train transformer models (125M-1B parameters) on four diverse text domains: Wikipedia (general knowledge), GitHub (code), arXiv (scientific), and OpenWebText (conversational). Establish three baselines: (a) uniform mixing, (b) static expert-designed ratios, (c) gradient-based adaptive mixing (PiKE-style). 2. **Representation Metrics**: Implement three health metrics computed every N steps: (a) Attention Entropy: Mean entropy of attention weights across heads/layers for samples from each source, (b) Clustering Quality: Silhouette score of source-labeled embeddings in representation space, (c) Layer-wise Similarity: CKA between consecutive layer representations. 3. **Adaptive Mixing Algorithm**: Start with uniform mixing. Every K steps, compute health metrics per source. If a source shows: low attention entropy (<threshold), decrease sampling; high attention entropy, increase sampling; poor clustering (silhouette <0.3), increase sampling; high layer similarity (CKA >0.95), decrease sampling. Use exponential moving average for stability. 4. **Evaluation Protocol**: (a) Perplexity on held-out sets from each domain to measure balanced learning, (b) Downstream tasks: GLUE for general language, HumanEval for code, SciQ for scientific reasoning, (c) Representation quality: linear probing accuracy for domain classification, (d) Computational overhead measurement, (e) Ablation studies removing each metric individually. 5. **Analysis**: Visualize representation geometry via t-SNE at different training stages, plot attention entropy evolution per source, compare convergence speed and final performance against baselines, measure early detection capability by tracking when mixing adjustments occur versus when loss degradation appears.

## Risk Factors And Limitations

1. **Metric Selection**: The chosen representation metrics may not capture all aspects of learning health; some domains might require different indicators. Mitigation: Extensive ablation studies and potential for domain-specific metric customization. 2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: Thresholds for triggering mixing adjustments and update frequencies require tuning. Mitigation: Grid search on validation sets and sensitivity analysis. 3. **Computational Overhead**: While designed to be minimal, computing representation metrics adds some cost. Mitigation: Efficient implementation using sampling (not full dataset) and batched computation. 4. **Scale Limitations**: Experiments at 125M-1B parameters may not fully reflect dynamics at 10B+ scale. Mitigation: Focus on demonstrating the principle; discuss scaling considerations. 5. **Generalization**: Method developed for text may need adaptation for multimodal settings. Mitigation: Clearly scope to language models; discuss extensions in future work. 6. **Metric Reliability**: Early in training, representations may be too noisy for reliable metrics. Mitigation: Implement warm-up period with static mixing before enabling adaptive adjustments. 7. **Overfitting to Metrics**: Optimizing for representation health might not always align with downstream performance. Mitigation: Include downstream evaluation throughout training, not just at the end.
