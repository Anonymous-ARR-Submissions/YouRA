## Name

symmetry_aware_weight_augmentation

## Title

Symmetry-Aware Augmentation for Data-Efficient Weight-Space Learning

## Short Hypothesis

Neural network weights exhibit well-defined symmetries (permutation, scaling, sign-flipping) that can be exploited as augmentation strategies to improve data efficiency and generalization in weight-space learning tasks. Unlike image augmentation which preserves semantic content, weight augmentation must preserve functional equivalence while increasing diversity in parameter space. We hypothesize that explicitly leveraging these symmetries as augmentation operations will significantly improve sample efficiency in weight-space learning compared to naive augmentation or no augmentation, particularly in low-data regimes.

## Related Work

This work distinguishes itself from existing literature in several key ways: (1) Unlike equivariant architectures (Dayan et al., 2026; Kohn et al., 2023) that build symmetry into model design, we focus on data augmentation strategies that can be applied to any weight-space learning method. (2) While Meynent et al. (2025) identify limitations of structural losses in weight-space autoencoders, they don't explore augmentation as a solution. (3) Recent work on weight-space generation (Erdogan, 2025; Tian et al., 2025) and model merging (Chaves et al., 2025) doesn't systematically study augmentation strategies. (4) Beiser et al. (2025) use augmentation for reverse-engineering but in a different context (input-output queries) and don't explore symmetry-preserving operations. Our work fills this gap by providing the first systematic study of symmetry-aware augmentation for weight-space learning across multiple downstream tasks.

## Abstract

Neural network weights are increasingly being treated as a new data modality, with applications in model property prediction, weight generation, and model merging. However, weight-space learning suffers from severe data inefficiency: publicly available model zoos contain far fewer samples than typical vision or language datasets. We propose to address this bottleneck through symmetry-aware augmentation strategies that leverage the inherent symmetries of neural network weights. Unlike naive augmentation (e.g., adding Gaussian noise), our approach exploits functional equivalences: permutation symmetries between neurons, scaling symmetries induced by activation functions, and sign-flipping symmetries in certain architectures. We develop a taxonomy of symmetry-preserving augmentation operations and systematically evaluate their impact on three representative weight-space learning tasks: (1) model property prediction (accuracy, generalization gap), (2) weight-space autoencoding, and (3) model merging. Our experiments span multiple architectures (MLPs, CNNs, ResNets) trained on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyImageNet. We demonstrate that symmetry-aware augmentation improves sample efficiency by 2-5x compared to baselines without augmentation, with particularly strong gains in low-data regimes (< 1000 models). Furthermore, we show that combining multiple symmetry operations yields compounding benefits, and that augmentation quality can be measured by the functional similarity between original and augmented weights. Our work provides practical guidelines for data augmentation in weight-space learning and opens new directions for improving data efficiency in this emerging field.

## Experiments

We will conduct three sets of experiments: **Experiment 1: Model Property Prediction.** Train regressors to predict test accuracy and generalization gap from weights. Dataset: 5000 CNNs trained on CIFAR-10 with varying hyperparameters. Augmentations tested: (A) None (baseline), (B) Gaussian noise, (C) Random permutation of neurons within layers, (D) Neuron-wise scaling, (E) Combined symmetry operations. Architecture: Graph Neural Network operating on weight tensors. Metrics: Mean Absolute Error (MAE) on held-out test set, sample efficiency curves (performance vs. training set size from 100 to 4000 models). **Experiment 2: Weight-Space Autoencoding.** Train autoencoders to compress and reconstruct network weights. Dataset: 3000 ResNet-18 models trained on CIFAR-100. Augmentations: Same as Experiment 1. Architecture: Permutation-equivariant autoencoder baseline. Metrics: (1) Reconstruction error (L2 distance in weight space), (2) Functional similarity (correlation between original and reconstructed model predictions on validation set), (3) Test accuracy degradation of reconstructed models. **Experiment 3: Model Merging.** Learn to merge task-specific models for multi-task learning. Dataset: 500 models per task across 5 CIFAR-100 superclasses. Augmentations: Apply symmetry operations before merging. Method: Weight averaging with learned coefficients. Metrics: Multi-task accuracy, forgetting metric. **Implementation Details:** All augmentations will maintain functional equivalence by tracking neuron correspondences across layers. We will implement efficient batched augmentation operations and validate functional equivalence by measuring output distribution similarity (KL divergence) on a fixed validation set. Statistical significance will be assessed using 5 random seeds and paired t-tests.

## Risk Factors And Limitations

(1) **Computational cost**: Generating augmented weight samples requires careful implementation to avoid memory bottlenecks when working with large models. Mitigation: Focus on smaller architectures (ResNet-18, small CNNs) and implement efficient batched operations. (2) **Symmetry breaking**: Some architectures (e.g., batch normalization, skip connections) may have limited symmetries. Mitigation: Characterize which symmetries apply to which architectures and provide architecture-specific guidelines. (3) **Functional equivalence verification**: Ensuring augmented weights are truly functionally equivalent is non-trivial. Mitigation: Develop robust testing protocols using output distribution similarity on validation sets. (4) **Limited generalization**: Results may not transfer to very large models (e.g., LLMs) due to resource constraints. Mitigation: Clearly scope experiments to vision models and discuss scaling considerations. (5) **Task dependency**: Optimal augmentation strategies may be task-dependent. Mitigation: Test across multiple tasks and provide analysis of when each augmentation type is most beneficial. (6) **Dataset bias**: Model zoos may have biased distributions of hyperparameters. Mitigation: Control for hyperparameter diversity in our generated datasets and report distributional statistics.
