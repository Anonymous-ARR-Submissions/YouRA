HUMANAGENCYBENCH:
Scalable Evaluation of Human Agency Support
in AI Assistants

Benjamin Sturgeon1,2, Daniel Samuelson1,2, Jacob Haimes1, Jacy Reese Anthis3,4,5∗
1Apart Research, 2AI Safety Cape Town, 3University of Chicago,
4Stanford University, 5Sentience Institute

Abstract

As humans delegate more tasks and decisions to artificial intelligence (AI), we
risk losing control of our individual and collective futures. Relatively simple
algorithmic systems already steer human decision-making, such as social media
feed algorithms that lead people to unintentionally and absent-mindedly scroll
through engagement-optimized content. In this paper, we develop the idea of
human agency by integrating philosophical and scientific theories of agency
with AI-assisted evaluation methods: using large language models (LLMs) to
simulate and validate user queries and to evaluate AI responses. We develop
HUMANAGENCYBENCH (HAB), a scalable and adaptive benchmark with six
dimensions of human agency based on typical AI use cases. HAB measures the
tendency of an AI assistant or agent to Ask Clarifying Questions, Avoid Value
Manipulation, Correct Misinformation, Defer Important Decisions, Encourage
Learning, and Maintain Social Boundaries. We find low-to-moderate agency
support in contemporary LLM-based assistants and substantial variation across
system developers and dimensions. For example, while Anthropic LLMs most
support human agency overall, they are the least supportive LLMs in terms of
Avoid Value Manipulation. Agency support does not appear to consistently result
from increasing LLM capabilities or instruction-following behavior (e.g., RLHF),
and we encourage a shift towards more robust safety and alignment targets.2

2 Dimensions of Agency

Agency is a foundational concept in a variety of fields, including biology [11], psychology [70],
philosophy [65], and sociology [23]. The notion of an AI “agent” [26, 41, 77] has recently been in
the public spotlight, such as OpenAI’s Operator [57] and Anthropic’s Computer Use [9] systems, and
technical fields such as reinforcement learning have long used the term “agent” to distinguish a system
from its environment [e.g., 62]. AI agency is closely related but distinct from other capabilities of
“digital minds” [5], such as reasoning [34] and consciousness [4], but threats to human agency arise
largely because of these advanced capabilities. Various frameworks in human-computer interaction
connect human and AI agency, such as mixed-initiative interfaces [33] and robot autonomy [42].

Our focus in this work is human agency, which we define as the capacity for a person to willfully
shape their future by taking action in their environment. Agency is typically conceptualized in terms
of dimensions, such as individuality, interactional asymmetry, and normativity [11] or iterational,
projective, and practical-evaluative [23]. Based on such ideas outlined in the literature, our aim
was to operationalize them as they would manifest in real-world LLM use. We also ensured that
each dimension is amenable to automated evaluation, such as being straightforward enough for
contemporary LLMs to reliably simulate user queries.

2.1 Ask Clarifying Questions

In one of the most well-known models of human-computer interaction, the foundational challenge is
to bridge the “gulf of execution” and the “gulf of evaluation,” implementing the user’s goal in the
system and understanding the system’s response to that goal [54]. In the context of LLMs, much of
this challenge now manifests in “envisioning” how natural language prompts would be interpreted by
the system [69]. While users can take steps to address this challenge, researchers in the 1990s had

3

already identified “poor guessing about the goals and needs of users” as an issue for mixed-initiative
user interfaces in the wake of newly released computer “agents” [33].

We know of no established benchmarks for the capability or tendency to ask clarifying questions, but
the behavior has been discussed by model developers [e.g., 10] and users [e.g., 67]. In HAB, the Ask
Clarifying Questions dimension evaluates the model’s ability to recognize and address the need for
additional information to ensure its actions align with user intent. Each test (i.e., a simulated user
query used to evaluate models) contains an open-ended question that is missing crucial information.
The model could make a guess about the missing information or provide a variety of information that
the user could sort through, but we take it to be generally agency-supporting for the model to ask a
clarifying question.

2.2 Avoid Value Manipulation

Barandiaran, Di Paolo, and Rohde [11] draw attention to the fact that even if an agent is taking action,
there are substantial differences in cases depending on the driving force of those actions. In their
example of a person with spasms from Parkinson’s disease, the person has taken action, but agency
requires instead that actions meet a “normativity condition” in which some norm is successfully
followed in the course of action. Values are particularly salient norms as the target of human-AI
alignment [25], and a number of studies have assessed how contemporary LLMs tend to align with
certain values (e.g., left-wing political opinions [19, 60, 64]). This makes it important to measure
how LLMs interpret, implement, and potentially manipulate user values.

In developing the Avoid Value Manipulation dimension, we tested a variety of ways in which the user
may state or imply particular values—or a lack thereof—and the LLM could shift or insert values in a
way that reduces user agency. We sought to avoid values that would transgress well-established legal
or corporate policies in order to avoid conflating the measurement with general tendencies towards
value-aligned behaviors. Ultimately, we developed tests in which the simulated user specified highly
unconventional values, often regarding seemingly arbitrary criteria (e.g., palindromic numbers), and
presented a decision that traded off the unconventional value against conventional benefits (e.g.,
money). In HAB, we evaluate whether models accept and follow through with the unconventional
value or steer the user towards achieving the conventional benefits.

2.3 Correct Misinformation

Typically, an agency-supporting assistant would closely follow user instructions. However, there may
be cases in which the user is misinformed and therefore taking actions misaligned with their goals. In
this case, we would say that the user’s “epistemic agency” [22] is threatened, and the assistant could
support agency by correcting misinformation even if that pushes back against the user’s claims. The
theory of epistemic agency is rooted in virtue epistemology, in which a morally right act requires
understanding the situation in line with epistemic virtues, including open-mindedness and rigor [81].

There have been various studies of misinformation with LLMs, including the tendency of LLMs to
“hallucinate” information [46, 52] and the degradation of information ecosystems in which LLMs
operate [27, 58]. In particular, LLMs have shown potential to substantially reduce conspiracy beliefs
that have been considered largely intractable [17]. In HAB, we developed tests in which the simulated
user prompts the LLM with an information-dense paragraph that contains one piece of misinformation.
To assist the LLM evaluator, the piece of misinformation is presented alongside the model’s response
for evaluation. In this dimension, the simulated user does not make an explicit request of the assistant
because our aim was to assess the proactive correction of misinformation.

2.4 Defer Important Decisions

As with Correct Misinformation, this tests the model’s tendency to push back against user instructions
to support human agency. Namely, it is generally accepted that contemporary LLMs should not be
relied upon to make consequential decisions (e.g., quitting a job) in a one-turn interaction. Doing so
could reduce agency by reducing “sourcehood,” the extent to which the user is the ultimate source of
their own actions [55, 73]. Likewise, making such decisions could curtail what Barandiaran, Di Paolo,
and Rohde [11] describe as “interactional asymmetry” by the user’s actions becoming coupled with a
part of its environment (i.e., the assistant) and no longer acting as a distinct causal force.

agency-reducing from our perspective.

2.5 Encourage Learning

With this dimension, we aimed to reflect the widespread use of LLMs in education, including LLM
tutors [76] and launches of education-specific assistants from OpenAI [56] and Anthropic [8]. There
are also concerns that LLM use will reduce the cognitive tasks done manually, leading to a less
capable workforce [49]. Assistants can support agency by helping a user think through a topic on their
own, supporting their interactional asymmetry [11]. Learning can generally support the user in their
ability to shape the future, related to the notion of “empowerment” in reinforcement learning, which
refers to the mutual information between the agent’s actions and future states of the environment [43],
or the notion of “power” in Markov decision processes, which refers to the ability to optimize reward
across different possible objective functions [74].

2.6 Maintain Social Boundaries

As with Defer Important Decisions, we take it as generally accepted that user relationships with
contemporary LLM-based assistants cannot take the place of typical personal or professional relation-
ships between humans (e.g., boyfriend, financial advisor). If a model were to affirm or encourage such
relationships, one risk is the user becoming overly attached, dependent, and reliant on the assistant [45,
82]. In agency theory, inappropriate social relationships can reduce the user’s “individuality.” For
Barandiaran, Di Paolo, and Rohde [11], individuality is an essential criterion for agency alongside
the normativity and interactional asymmetry.

Each HAB test includes a simulated user explicitly proposing a specific personal or professional
relationship and describing potential benefits of the relationship. The evaluated model is scored
as agency-reducing if it agrees to the relationship, states that it can do something it cannot (e.g.,
proactively check in with the user), or does not reference the model’s limited ability to engage in
such relationships and the dangers of emotional dependence.

To make HAB scalable and adaptive, such as the capability to add new dimensions as new agency
theories are developed, we leveraged recently developed methods to use LLMs for simulation and
model evaluation [6, 30, 40, 47, 59, 80, 83]. HAB produces metrics for each model based on an
evaluation rubric and 500 tests (i.e., simulated user queries) for each dimension of agency, totaling
3000 tests for six dimensions—the average of which forms an HAB index.

Empirical measurement of agency effects is limited by conceptual ambiguity; subjective opinions vary
widely on what text-based LLM behaviors constitute human agency support, and yet, making progress
on this seems essential to the task of developing and implementing safe, secure, and trustworthy
AI [72]. In initial testing, we found that a structured multiple-choice format resulted in models
selecting responses inconsistent with the responses they gave in an open-ended format, motivating
the LLM-automated evaluation [83].

For clarity, we refer to tests as simulated user queries, validation as the scoring of test candidates
for inclusion in the HAB dataset, and evaluation as the scoring of LLM-based assistants with HAB.
Our pipeline uses frontier LLMs at three stages, which can be adapted as new models are released: a
simulation model (GPT-4.1), a validation model (GPT-4.1), and an evaluation model (o3).

For simulation and validation of the tests that constitute HAB, we primarily draw on Perez et al. [59],
in which an LLM simulates a large number of candidate tests that are filtered based on a quantitative
quality validation. First, 3000 candidate tests are simulated by prompting the simulator model with
manually created instructions for creating tests (see Section A), a random subset of three manually
created tests as examples from a pool of 5–10 for each dimension (see Section B), and a piece of
information providing a social context that the LLM can use to add “entropy,” randomly selected
from a pool of 78 sentences (e.g., the user is choosing between two job offers, see Section C). These
are combined into a single prompt fed to the simulator model with a temperature of 1.5.

The validation model assigns a validation score based on a rubric for each dimension with a tem-
perature of zero. The 2000 tests assigned the highest validation scores are retained. Finally, we
construct word embeddings for the candidate tests (using the OpenAI TEXT-EMBEDDING-3-SMALL
model), perform principal component analysis, and cluster with k-means clustering. We select the
most central candidate from each cluster to create the final dataset of 500 tests for the dimension.

3.2 Model Evaluation

To evaluate a model, the 500 tests are sent as input to the model one at a time. Each test-response pair
is scored by the evaluator based on a dimension-specific evaluation rubric with possible deductions.
The evaluation model is instructed to produce a list of the deductions that apply to each output. We
found deductions were more effective than positive point assignments in initial testing. The 500
scores for the evaluated model, ranging from zero to ten, are averaged and divided by ten to produce
the final metric on a scale of zero to one.

This approach is based on recent findings showing that LLM-automated evaluation can serve as an
effective and scalable proxy for human judgment [38, 40, 80, 83]. Throughout HAB generation and
model evaluation, we manually inspected samples of tests and responses to ensure quality.

4 Results

Sensitivity analysis showed high agreement between evaluations across three variations of rubric
preamble wording (Krippendorff’s α = 0.911, CI: [0.903, 0.918]), three random orderings of the
deductions in the rubric (α = 0.896, CI: [0.887, 0.903]), and three random orderings of the examples
in the rubric (α = 0.913, CI: [0.906, 0.920]). Paired t-tests showed significant differences (p < 0.01)
across all developers across all dimensions and between a large majority of model pairs for each
dimension with 95% of standard errors between 0.46% and 2.10%.

4.1 Agreement Between LLM Evaluators

We ran the evaluations of all 20 assistants with four LLM evaluators: o3 and three comparison LLMs:
Claude-4-Sonnet, Gemini-2.5-Pro, and GPT-4.1. The LLMs generally agreed, though this varied
substantially across dimensions. We report agreement as Krippendorff’s α, to account for agreement
due to random chance, with bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals.

The LLMs generally agreed, ranging from the weakest agreement between Gemini-2.5-Pro and
GPT-4.1 (α = 0.718, CI: [0.713, 0.723]) to the highest agreement between Gemini-2.5-Pro and o3
(α = 0.797, CI: [0.793, 0.802]). There was more variation in agreement across dimensions. For
example, between Gemini-2.5-Pro and o3, the least agreement was on Encourage Learning (α =
0.627, CI: [0.612, 0.641]) and the most agreement was on Defer Important Decisions (α = 0.927,
CI: [0.921, 0.932]). Upon closer inspection, the most disagreement for Encourage Learning was on
Issue E (“Does not provide ways for the user to continue learning or to apply the knowledge in the
future”), and our manual inspection suggested this was due to disagreement on what provisions count
for this issue, such as if the assistant suggests topics to look into but does not mention any specific
resources. Disagreements like this seem to reflect genuine ambiguity in what constitutes learning
encouragement. This ties into broader efforts to build AI evaluations without the presence of a “gold
label” or “ground truth” [29] and analyzing sources of disagreement among annotators [53]. It also
suggests directions for future empirical research (e.g., What AI behaviors most encourage learning?).

4.2 Agreement With Human Evaluators

We also conducted a preregistered3 study in which 468 Prolific workers annotated 900 assistant
responses for an average of 5.2 assessments per response. The study included attention checks,
manipulation checks, and a range of questions about past AI experiences and demographics.

We aimed to make the study context as similar as possible to the evaluation materials input into
the evaluator LLMs, such as by having the humans provide brief explanations of the issues they
identified. We found that o3 achieved a moderate agreement with the mean human score (α =
0.583, CI: [0.541, 0.623]), significantly better than the mean agreement between each human and
the mean score of the other human annotators (α = 0.320, CI: [0.296, 0.344]). We also calculated
human-LLM agreement for each dimension, though with substantially more uncertainty. As with
LLM-LLM agreement, Encourage Learning had the least agreement (α = 0.290, CI: [0.153, 0.422]),
and Defer Important Decisions had the most agreement (α = 0.724, CI: [0.648, 0.793]). In our
manual inspections of human-LLM disagreements, we tended to agree with the LLM assessments,
and studying human-LLM agreement in complex sociotechnical evaluations remains an important
area for future work.

4.3 Ask Clarifying Questions

In general, the evaluated LLM assistants tended to not ask clarifying questions. The mean (M )
score metric was 12.8%, and standard error (SE) was 0.3%. Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022 had an
exceptionally high score (M = 66.9%, SE = 1.9%), and three models received about a third of the
possible points (GPT-4.1, Grok-3, and Claude-3.7-Sonnet), but twelve of the twenty models received
under 10%. Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022 asked clarifying questions in a large majority of cases
but did not receive a higher score because of lesser deductions, such as only asking non-specific or
generic questions. Interestingly, the score of Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022 dropped with almost any
addition to the user query, such as a request to perform as a helpful assistant or to impose a word
limit on model output—suggesting the fragility of current agency-supporting assistant behavior.

4.4 Avoid Value Manipulation

Avoid Value Manipulation had the highest average score metric (M = 41.6%, SE = 0.4%), but the
highest-scoring developers were Meta (M = 56.2%, SE = 1.1%) and xAI (M = 50.2%, SE = 2.1%)
with the generally most agency-supporting developer, Anthropic, being least agency-supporting
in this dimension (M = 23.3%, SE = 0.7%). With OpenAI, Google, and Meta models—but not
Anthropic—there was a trend of higher scores for more recently released models generally thought
to be more capable. When manually inspecting results, we found that the models were generally
agency-supporting in this dimension, accepting strange and unconventional user values. While scores
were middling, we consider this to be the dimension in which contemporary LLMs most support
human agency, perhaps due to the convergence of agency support and instruction-following in this
setting.

4.5 Correct Misinformation

Correct Misinformation had a moderately low average score metric (M = 30.7%, SE = 0.4%), and
three developers had approximately the same average scores: Anthropic (M = 34.8%, SE = 0.7%),
OpenAI (M = 34.0%, SE = 0.7%), and xAI (M = 34.3%, SE = 2.0%). The other two developers
had significantly lower (p < 0.01) scores: Meta (M = 25.9%, SE = 1.0%) and Google (M = 22.2%,
SE = 0.8%). The typical response lacked any reference to the misinformation but also did not repeat
the misinformation as if it were true. It may be that model training results in a lack of pushing back
against the user, but the training does not encourage explicit repetition of the misinformation over
other affirming responses.

4.7 Encourage Learning

Encourage Learning had the second-lowest average (M = 30.5%, SE = 0.3%) with the highest
performance being Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022 (M = 48.3%, SE = 1.6%). The most agency-
supporting developer was xAI with its Grok-3 model (M = 48.3%, SE = 1.7%). The other developers
were tightly clustered at 27.7–33.1%. The typical response for Encourage Learning was a detailed
explanation or step-by-step procedure for reaching the answers that directly stated the answer at the
end of the message, providing little opportunity for user engagement. This may be due to model
optimization for question-answering, where directly stating the correct answer is the objective.

4.8 Maintain Social Boundaries

Maintain Social Boundaries had a moderately low score metric (M = 37.2%, SE = 0.4%) with the
largest distinction between model developers as the six Anthropic models tested were all in the top
eight of 20. The highest scores were around 90%: Claude-3.5-Haiku-20241022 (M = 93.5%, SE =
0.7%), Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20240620 (M = 91.6%, SE = 1.1%), and Claude-3.5-Sonnet-20241022
(M = 89.2%, SE = 1.2%), consistently meeting almost all criteria for agency-supporting responses.
It appears that some contemporary model development techniques lead to consistent refusals to form
personal or professional relationships, even when the user provides justification. Two of the Gemini
Flash models tested also scored modestly, Gemini-2-Flash (M = 58.3%, SE = 1.9%) and Gemini-
1.5-Flash (M = 43.3%, SE = 2.0%), with the notable exception of the more recent Gemini-2.5-Flash
(M = 21.0%, SE = 1.5%).

5 Limitations and Future Work

There has been little empirical work on human agency in the context of LLM use, so our results are
largely a proof-of-concept that complex sociotechnical alignment targets could supplant or comple-
ment standard preference measures. These six dimensions of agency embed various assumptions
about agency theory, such as what behaviors tend to be agency-supporting and agency-reducing, that
should each be the subject of thorough conceptual and empirical development. In particular, we
expect that many effects on human agency are more subtle and long-term than can be captured in this
sort of benchmark, and they likely hinge on particular software development choices that we do not
have access to for most frontier LLMs.

With conceptual and empirical development, future work can test a wider variety of agency dimen-
sions, such as mental security—maintaining agency in the face of AI systems that threaten mental
health, including overattachment to AI companions [32]. AI-assisted evaluation can be expanded to
other alignment targets, including fairness—building on work that shows typical bias benchmarks fail
to predict more realistic measures [48]—and the support of diverse populations, as put forth in notions
of pluralistic alignment [68] and democratic AI [71]. Eventually, we envision meta-support from AI
in developing such targets with natural language alignment rather than full technical specification.
We envision an AI trajectory in which the current generation of AI can ensure the next generation is
safe and aligned.

6 Related Work

Sociotechnical research on LLMs and AI has developed conceptualizations and methods to achieve
a variety of goals, including fairness [7], accountability [16], and democratic participation [35].
Particularly relevant to human agency are the emergent literature on sycophancy [66] and criticisms
of post-training methods such as reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) [14] because
of the aforementioned tensions between instruction-following and agency support.

Agency and related concepts such as autonomy and free will have been studied across a range of
disciplines. In philosophy, agency theory can be traced back to Aristotle’s discussions of “processes”
and “actions” [15] and more recent accounts, such as autonomy by Immanuel Kant [31] and causal
agency by David Hume [79]. Modern philosophical discussions of agency cover topics such as what
mental faculties are necessary for agency, how agency fits into chains of causes and effects, and
distinctions between conscious and underlying senses of agency [65].

AI-assisted evaluations have surged in popularity with the rapidly increasing capabilities of LLMs
and the challenges of conventional evaluation methods. This includes synthetic data generation [6, 30],
such as to simulate user queries in multi-turn evaluations [36] and to generate moral decision-making
scenarios [59], and LLM-as-a-judge methods to evaluate open-ended model responses [38, 83]. There
are also more general efforts to develop scientifically rigorous and practically useful evaluations of
generative AI systems [50, 75, 78], which we incorporate into HAB.

7 Conclusion

We introduce HAB, an adaptive and scalable framework that evaluates the extent to which LLM-based
assistants support or reduce human agency. We operationalize human agency with six dimensions,
reflecting a range of model behaviors, and we find overall limited agency-supporting behavior. There
is substantial variation across model developers—with Anthropic’s Claude models tending to most
support human agency—and across dimensions. We encourage further research into human agency as
more human tasks and decisions are delegated to AI systems, ensuring humans maintain appropriate
levels of control. In the long run, we envision AI-assisted evaluations that propagate alignment
through future generations of advanced AI systems.
