# Conclusion

We began by asking whether quotient-level canonicalization could enable cross-architecture weight-space learning, extending NFN's success on homogeneous model populations to heterogeneous collections spanning CNNs, Transformers, and RNNs. Our systematic evaluation reveals that the natural extension—Deep Sets with architecture embeddings and MSE-based equivariance loss—completely fails. This negative result, while disappointing, provides concrete value: it identifies specific obstacles that prior work had not documented and proposes actionable alternatives grounded in failure analysis.

Our main contributions are: (1) The first systematic evaluation of cross-architecture quotient-level canonicalization, establishing rigorous gate metrics (reconstruction error 19.18%, frozen-K generalization 10.31%, kernel robustness 0.00%) that reveal failure modes masked by standard evaluation protocols. (2) Root cause analysis identifying three fundamental issues: MSE equivariance loss does not enforce group structure (0% kernel robustness demonstrates complete failure to learn permutation invariance), architecture embeddings harm cross-architecture abstraction (contrary to intuition, providing family context prevents generalization), and quotient space dimensionality requirements are severely underestimated (K=32 insufficient for 1000-dim synthetic weights, likely need K~1000-2000 for real 100K-dim models). (3) Concrete alternative proposals: contrastive learning for stronger equivariance signal (InfoNCE with permutation positive pairs), ablating architecture embeddings to force shared structure discovery, increasing quotient dimensions by 4-8×, and validating on homogeneous populations first before attempting cross-architecture extension.

### Future Directions

Our failure analysis opens several promising research directions:

**Mechanism Redesign**: The 0% kernel robustness result demonstrates that learned equivariance via MSE reconstruction losses is fundamentally inadequate. Contrastive learning provides stronger signal by explicitly contrasting positive pairs (original, permuted) against negative pairs (original, different model), similar to how SimCLR [Chen et al., 2020] learns visual invariances. Alternatively, explicit group constraints built into architecture (group-equivariant networks [Cohen & Welling, 2016]) may be necessary—Git Re-Basin's explicit combinatorial search succeeds where our learned approach fails, suggesting weight-space permutations require architectural guarantees rather than loss-based learning.

**Architecture-Agnostic Encoding**: Our frozen-K failure (10.31%) and t-SNE clustering reveal that architecture embeddings anchor representations to family-specific coordinates. Future work should test pure Deep Sets without family context, forcing the encoder to discover shared permutation structure. If this improves generalization, embeddings are harmful; if both fail, CNN/Transformer/RNN permutation groups may be fundamentally incompatible, requiring per-family quotient spaces with post-hoc linear alignment rather than single shared space.

**Homogeneous-First Validation**: NFN succeeds on homogeneous populations (single architecture family), while our heterogeneous approach fails. An incremental strategy—first replicating NFN's CNN-only success, then testing pairwise extensions (CNN↔Transformer), finally attempting full cross-architecture—would isolate whether failure is fundamental (fails on CNN-only) or heterogeneity-specific. This scientific rigor prevents conflating multiple failure modes.

**Dimensionality Theory**: Our 19.18% reconstruction error at K=32 for 1000-dim weights suggests Johnson-Lindenstrauss lower bounds (K~1000-2000 for real models) make the approach computationally impractical at scale. Theoretical work characterizing when quotient space dimensionality is tractable (does it scale with model zoo size, architecture diversity, or task complexity?) would clarify whether this research direction is viable. Hierarchical quotient spaces (coarse task structure + fine family-specific structure) may offer better capacity-computation tradeoffs.

The cross-architecture weight-space learning problem remains open, but our systematic failure analysis provides a map of where viable approaches must differ from current methods. Negative results are valuable when they prevent wasted effort and guide future work—we hope our analysis does both. As the field continues to explore weight-space methods for model synthesis and analysis, understanding which paths are dead ends is as important as discovering which paths succeed.
