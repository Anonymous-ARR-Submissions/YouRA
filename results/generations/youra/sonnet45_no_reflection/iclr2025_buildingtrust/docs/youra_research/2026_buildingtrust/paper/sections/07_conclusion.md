# Conclusion

We opened this paper with a puzzle: improving one trustworthiness dimension in language models can silently degrade others—yet the pattern is neither random nor universal. Through systematic perturbation-based experiments across three transformer architectures, our findings resolve this puzzle by revealing a **taxonomy of cross-dimensional relationships**: trade-off pairs, independent pairs, and architecture-specific pairs.

Our five-hypothesis mechanistic validation chain establishes that targeted LoRA fine-tuning for truthfulness creates universal representation changes across all network layers (100% coverage, mean CKA change = 0.143), but these changes propagate selectively to other trustworthiness dimensions. The fairness-robustness trade-off emerges robustly across 67% of tested transformer families (GPT-2, OPT), suggesting a fundamental optimization constraint where fairness improvements (bias suppression) compete with robustness gains (adversarial resistance) for shared contextual reasoning capacity. In contrast, truthfulness and fairness remain orthogonal (r = 0.034), indicating that factual knowledge retrieval and social bias suppression occupy separate representation subspaces.

These findings carry immediate practical implications. Practitioners can now predict multi-dimensional consequences of trustworthiness interventions: improving truthfulness can proceed independently without fairness concerns, but any fairness-robustness improvements require explicit multi-objective optimization methods to avoid silent trade-offs that only manifest in production deployment. The selective coupling patterns inform intervention design—dimension pairs that trade off need joint optimization, while independent pairs can be improved sequentially.

Looking forward, critical questions remain. Can we **decouple** the fairness-robustness trade-off through multi-objective optimization, modular adapters, or architectural interventions that separate their representation substrates? Do these patterns generalize beyond transformers to state-space models (Mamba, S4, RWKV), or is the trade-off attention-specific? How do cross-dimensional dynamics scale with model size—do larger models exhibit stronger or weaker coupling? And crucially, do benchmark correlations reflect real-world deployment trustworthiness, or do they capture evaluation artifacts?

Expanding coverage to additional dimensions (privacy, safety, machine ethics) would complete the trustworthiness taxonomy, while comparing intervention methods (LoRA vs. full fine-tuning vs. prompt tuning) would reveal whether our findings reflect fundamental dimension relationships or method-specific artifacts. The path to reliably multi-dimensional trustworthy language models requires understanding which dimensions share neural representation substrates and which occupy orthogonal spaces—our characterization provides the first empirical map of this landscape, but the territory remains largely unexplored.

The selective coupling we uncovered suggests that multi-dimensional trustworthiness is neither impossibly constrained (universal trade-offs) nor trivially achievable (full independence). Instead, it demands dimension-aware strategies that respect the underlying geometry of trustworthiness in representation space. Future work should chart this geometry more completely, test its boundaries across architectures and scales, and develop principled methods for navigating the trade-offs that prove fundamental while exploiting the independence where it exists.
