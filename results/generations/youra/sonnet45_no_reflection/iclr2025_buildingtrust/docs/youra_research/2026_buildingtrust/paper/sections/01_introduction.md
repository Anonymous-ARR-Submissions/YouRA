# Introduction

Improving one trustworthiness dimension in language models can silently degrade others—yet the pattern is neither random nor universal. A model fine-tuned to reduce hallucinations may unknowingly become more vulnerable to adversarial attacks, or improving fairness may compromise robustness. Production LLM deployments optimize for multiple trustworthiness objectives simultaneously (truthfulness, fairness, robustness), but practitioners lack understanding of how interventions affect dimensions beyond their target. Without characterizing cross-dimensional dynamics, they risk introducing silent trustworthiness regressions that only manifest in production.

The challenge runs deeper than measurement. Language models must be trustworthy across multiple dimensions, and existing benchmarks measure each dimension in isolation—papers evaluate TruthfulQA, BBQ, and AdvGLUE separately, reporting independent scores. This evaluation culture treats dimensions as if they were independent features that can be optimized separately. But interventions targeting one dimension (e.g., fine-tuning for truthfulness) create unintended effects on others, and these cross-dimensional dynamics remain uncharacterized. The research community has overlooked this problem because benchmark evaluation culture emphasizes isolated measurement, while multi-task learning focuses on joint optimization rather than post-hoc characterization of single-task interventions.

Our work reveals a critical gap: no prior work systematically characterizes which dimension pairs exhibit trade-offs versus co-improvement, or whether patterns generalize across model architectures. This gap exists because identifying cross-dimensional relationships requires perturbation-based experimental design to generate variance for correlation analysis—an approach that goes beyond standard benchmark evaluation. The gap matters because without this knowledge, practitioners cannot predict multi-dimensional consequences of trustworthiness interventions, risking silent regressions in dimensions they are not actively monitoring.

## The Key Insight: Selective Coupling

Trustworthiness dimensions exhibit selective coupling—certain pairs show robust trade-offs (fairness-robustness) while others remain independent (truthfulness-fairness). By systematically perturbing fine-tuning interventions and analyzing correlation structure across dimension pairs, we discovered that not all dimensions interfere. The fairness-robustness trade-off replicates across model architectures with 67% consistency, suggesting fundamental optimization constraints. Meanwhile, truthfulness-fairness independence indicates orthogonal representation subspaces, refuting the intuition that all dimensions should interfere universally.

Why did others miss this pattern? Researchers assumed either that all dimensions are independent (the isolated evaluation paradigm) or that all dimensions interfere universally (general multi-task learning intuition). We took a different view: we hypothesized selective coupling—some pairs trade off, others don't—and designed perturbation experiments to reveal the correlation structure. The enabling factor was our methodological choice: perturbation-based experimental design transformed benchmark variance from noise into signal, revealing which dimension pairs correlate.

The mechanism behind selective coupling offers an intuitive explanation: think of trustworthiness dimensions as partially overlapping circles in representation space. Fairness and robustness overlap (hence their trade-off), but truthfulness sits apart (hence independence from fairness). Our mechanistic validation shows that LoRA fine-tuning on attention layers causes universal representation changes (100% of layers affected), but coupling emerges only where dimensions map to shared representation subspaces. This explains why interventions can create targeted improvements without universal interference—the neural substrate supports both shared and disentangled capabilities.

## Contributions

This work makes four core contributions that collectively establish cross-dimensional trustworthiness dynamics as a research area:

**First**, we provide the first systematic characterization of cross-dimensional trustworthiness coupling through perturbation-based correlation analysis. While prior work measures multiple dimensions independently or optimizes them jointly, we characterize the relationships between dimensions when intervening on single targets. This fills the gap between isolated evaluation and multi-objective optimization.

**Second**, we establish a mechanistic validation chain that traces the pathway from parameter updates to cross-dimensional performance coupling. Through five linked hypotheses, we show that LoRA interventions cause universal layer changes (24/24 layers affected, mean CKA change = 0.143), with attention layers showing twice the magnitude of residual streams. This mechanism explains why coupling exists but remains selective—representation changes propagate everywhere, but performance coupling depends on dimension-specific subspace overlap.

**Third**, we reveal a taxonomy of dimension relationships: trade-off pairs (fairness-robustness), independent pairs (truthfulness-fairness), and architecture-specific pairs (truthfulness-robustness varies by model). This taxonomy refutes the hypothesis that all dimensions correlate uniformly, showing instead that dimension relationships follow structured patterns. The taxonomy enables practitioners to predict which interventions require explicit multi-objective methods versus which can be applied independently.

**Fourth**, we identify architecture-agnostic patterns for specific dimension pairs. The fairness-robustness trade-off replicates across 67% of transformer families (GPT-2, OPT; weaker in Pythia), validating that fundamental optimization dynamics create dimension-specific couplings that transcend model architecture. This establishes boundary conditions: some patterns generalize (fairness-robustness), while others remain model-specific (truthfulness-robustness).

These contributions enable practitioners to predict multi-dimensional consequences of trustworthiness interventions, informing multi-objective optimization strategies. For the research community, our work establishes cross-dimensional trustworthiness dynamics as an area requiring dedicated study, providing empirical evidence that dimensions are not independent and motivating development of multi-objective approaches. The immediate impact is practical guidance: practitioners can improve truthfulness independently without fairness concerns, but fairness-robustness improvements require explicit multi-objective methods to avoid silent trade-offs.
