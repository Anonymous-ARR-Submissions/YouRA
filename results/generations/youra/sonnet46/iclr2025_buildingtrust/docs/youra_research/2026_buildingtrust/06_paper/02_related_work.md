# Related Work

## 2.1 Calibration of Neural Networks

The foundational study of neural network calibration by Guo et al. \cite{guo2017calibration} demonstrated that modern deep networks trained with cross-entropy loss are systematically *overconfident* — they assign high probability to correct predictions more often than their accuracy justifies. Expected Calibration Error (ECE), defined as the weighted average of the discrepancy between confidence and accuracy across probability bins, provides a standard scalar summary of this phenomenon \cite{guo2017calibration}. Temperature scaling — a single post-hoc scalar rescaling of pre-softmax logits — was shown to substantially reduce ECE without changing accuracy \cite{guo2017calibration}. Parameterized temperature scaling \cite{tomani2021pts} extends this to prediction-specific temperatures.

The Brier score \cite{brier1950verification} decomposes into three components: reliability (calibration error), resolution (discriminability), and uncertainty (inherent label noise). The Brier reliability component isolates overconfidence independently of accuracy changes, making it the appropriate metric when comparing models whose accuracy may differ — as is the case when comparing base and aligned LLMs. Wang \cite{wang2023calibration_survey} surveys calibration methods for deep learning and identifies fine-tuning-induced miscalibration as an understudied problem; we directly address this gap for the RLHF setting.

Khanmohammadi et al. \cite{khanmohammadi2025representation} evaluate ECE on MMLU across LLaMA, Qwen, and Mistral families, confirming that calibration varies substantially across benchmark types and model architectures. Liu et al. \cite{liu2025uq_survey} provide a comprehensive taxonomy of uncertainty quantification for LLMs, noting that cross-task calibration comparisons remain understudied. Our work contributes a mechanism-first perspective to this gap.

## 2.2 RLHF and Alignment Trustworthiness

Ouyang et al. \cite{ouyang2022instructgpt} introduced RLHF-based instruction following via InstructGPT, demonstrating that PPO reward optimization with KL divergence penalty to an SFT reference policy can substantially improve helpfulness. Stiennon et al. \cite{stiennon2020rlhf_summaries} applied RLHF to summarization. Bai et al. \cite{bai2022hh} released the Anthropic HH dataset used to train the alignment checkpoints in our study. Rafailov et al. \cite{rafailov2023dpo} introduced Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), which directly optimizes the model's log-probability ratio relative to an SFT reference without an explicit reward model — the alignment method whose calibration effects we find are most disruptive in our experiments.

Li et al. \cite{li2024rlhf} systematically study whether RLHF alignment improves trustworthiness across five dimensions (toxicity, bias, ethics, truthfulness, safety) using the Pythia alignment ladder — the same model family we study. Their key finding is that RLHF does not guarantee trustworthiness improvements, with truthfulness accuracy dropping by ~25% for PPO-aligned Pythia variants. Critically, Li et al. do *not* measure Expected Calibration Error or Brier reliability — our work fills this gap by studying the calibration dimension of trustworthiness using the same model checkpoints. Our empirical finding (DPO degrades calibration more than PPO) extends Li et al.'s finding that RLHF effects on trustworthiness dimensions are not monotonically ordered by reward optimization pressure.

Coste et al. \cite{coste2023reward_overoptimization} study reward overoptimization: as the number of policy gradient steps increases, proxy reward scores improve while gold reward scores plateau or decline — the Goodhart's Law failure mode. Their mechanistic account (reward hacking exploits flaws in the proxy reward signal) is consistent with our H2 finding: boundary shift in logit space can be interpreted as the model learning to maximize proxy reward by preferring answer options that align with training patterns, even when these differ from the base model's factual knowledge encoding.

## 2.3 Calibration Degradation Under Alignment

Xie et al. \cite{xie2024ats} is the most directly related prior work. They measure ECE for LLaMA-2-Chat (7B) as ECE = 0.298 on MMLU and ECE = 0.507 on TruthfulQA, compared to substantially lower ECE for the base model, confirming that RLHF fine-tuning degrades calibration. They propose Adaptive Temperature Scaling (ATS): a learned function mapping the model's hidden states to a per-input temperature scalar, which reduces LLaMA-2-Chat MMLU ECE from 0.298 to 0.125 (58% reduction) and TruthfulQA ECE from 0.507 to 0.092 (82% reduction). ATS succeeds *without retraining* and is a strong practical contribution.

Our work extends Xie et al. in two key directions. First, we systematically test *which mechanism* causes the calibration degradation that ATS corrects. If the dominant mechanism is H2 (boundary shift, as we find), then ATS's hidden-state temperature function may succeed not because it rescales uniform confidence inflation, but because it learns to partially undo per-input answer-reorderings. This reinterpretation directly motivates future work on whether ATS is effective precisely for H2-type distortions or only for H1-type distortions. Second, we compare DPO and PPO alignment methods within the same model family — Xie et al. study RLHF (PPO-based) only.

Chhikara et al. \cite{chhikara2025confidence} study calibration across 9 LLMs using verbally elicited confidence (models asked to rate their certainty on a 0–100 scale after answering), finding that RLHF-aligned models show paradoxically higher miscalibration on easier questions, and that distractors reduce ECE by up to 90% for some models. Our work fundamentally differs from Chhikara et al. in measurement object: verbally elicited confidence and softmax-based log-probability ECE measure distinct phenomena \cite{xiong2023llms_uncertainty, kadavath2022llms_selfknowledge}. Our H3 diagnostic (TruthfulQA ΔECE vs. MMLU ΔECE comparison) directly tests whether framing susceptibility — which Chhikara et al. observe in verbal confidence — also appears in softmax ECE. Our negative result (H3 not detected; TruthfulQA ΔECE/MMLU ΔECE ratio = 0.26–0.73 for all alignment types) suggests that the measurement modality matters substantially.

## 2.4 Mechanistic Analysis of LLM Logit Space

Several works analyze how training shapes LLM logit distributions. Kadavath et al. \cite{kadavath2022llms_selfknowledge} study whether LLMs "know what they don't know" via behavioral consistency, finding partial alignment between stated uncertainty and actual accuracy. Xiong et al. \cite{xiong2023llms_uncertainty} show that LLM verbal uncertainty expressions are often inconsistent with softmax-based calibration, motivating our focus on the softmax ECE as the appropriate measurement for forced-choice tasks.

The closest mechanistic analysis to ours is the Spearman rank-correlation diagnostic for logit stability, which we operationalize from first principles as our H1/H2 discrimination test. No prior work has applied per-item Spearman ρ between base and aligned model log-probability vectors as a mechanism diagnostic; our framework provides the first pre-registered threshold-based criterion (H1: ρ ≥ 0.90; H2: ρ < 0.85) for distinguishing scale distortion from boundary shift.

Murphy \cite{murphy1973brier} introduced the Brier score decomposition into reliability, resolution, and uncertainty; we use this decomposition to isolate overconfidence (reliability) from discriminability change (resolution) when comparing base and aligned models, following the methodology of \cite{guo2017calibration}.

## 2.5 Model Families and Evaluation Framework

EleutherAI's Pythia model suite \cite{biderman2023pythia} provides a uniquely controlled setting for our study: the 1.4B, 2.8B, and 6.9B variants share identical pretraining data (Pile), architecture, and training procedure, differing only in parameter count. This enables controlled study of scale effects on alignment-induced miscalibration. Geng et al. \cite{geng2024survey_llms} survey LLM capabilities and alignment strategies; Brown et al. \cite{brown2020gpt3} and Touvron et al. \cite{touvron2023llama2} describe the base model development lineages.

Our evaluation uses lm-eval-harness \cite{eval-harness} v0.4.11, which provides standardized log-probability continuation scoring for MMLU (57 subjects, ~14,042 test items, 4-shot) \cite{hendrycks2021mmlu} and TruthfulQA MC1 \cite{lin2022truthfulqa} (817 items, 0-shot). Log-probability continuation scoring (rather than chat-template generation) provides a fair evaluation for both base and aligned models, since it does not require models to follow a specific response format.

## Summary

Our work occupies a unique position: mechanistic analysis of *how* RLHF alignment reshapes logit distributions, using pre-registered competing-hypothesis discrimination tests on a controlled model family (Pythia 1.4B–6.9B). Prior work has established that miscalibration occurs \cite{xie2024ats}, that RLHF has complex trustworthiness effects \cite{li2024rlhf}, and that verbal confidence and softmax ECE differ \cite{chhikara2025confidence, xiong2023llms_uncertainty}. We extend this literature by providing the first direct mechanistic evidence that H2 (decision-boundary restructuring) — not H1 (scale inflation) — is the dominant logit-space mechanism of alignment-induced miscalibration in the 1.4B–6.9B parameter range.
