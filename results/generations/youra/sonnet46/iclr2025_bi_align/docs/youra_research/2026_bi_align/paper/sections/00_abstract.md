# Abstract

Do humans adapt to better AI assistants — and if so, how? We address this question by measuring semantic accommodation in human-AI conversations from the Anthropic HH-RLHF dataset, where conversations are stratified across three reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) alignment quality tiers. We introduce C_sem, a training-free SBERT-based metric that quantifies interaction-specific semantic accommodation by subtracting a KNN topic-matched baseline from turn-pair cosine similarity, isolating alignment above topical coherence. Our analysis of 155,362 conversation pairs reveals that humans exhibit robust semantic accommodation to their AI partners (Cohen's d = 1.998 vs. random baseline), scaling monotonically with RLHF alignment quality (Jonckheere-Terpstra p = 0.001, confirmed across three SBERT architectures). Humans accommodate more to AI than AI to humans — consistent with power asymmetry theory — in all 9 tier × model conditions. Yet within-conversation quality discrimination is definitively falsified as the mechanism: human follow-up turns are paradoxically more similar to rejected AI responses than approved ones. Our findings establish that RLHF quality propagates bidirectionally, shaping human semantic behavior at the population level through distributional enrichment rather than within-exchange quality perception.
