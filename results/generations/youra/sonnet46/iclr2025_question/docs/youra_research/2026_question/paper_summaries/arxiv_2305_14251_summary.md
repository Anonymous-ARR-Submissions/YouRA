# FActScore: Fine-grained Atomic Evaluation of Factual Precision in Long Form Text Generation

## Key Metadata
- **Authors:** Sewon Min et al.
- **Year:** 2023
- **Venue:** arXiv
- **Core Contribution:** Introduction of FACTSCORE, an evaluation metric for the factual precision of long-form text generated by language models, through the assessment of atomic facts against a reliable knowledge source.

## Section Summaries

### Abstract
Evaluating the factuality of long-form text generated by large language models (LMs) is non-trivial because (1) generations often contain a mixture of supported and unsupported pieces of information, making binary judgments of quality inadequate, and (2) human evaluation is time-consuming and costly. In this paper, we introduce FACTSCORE, a new evaluation that breaks a generation into a series of atomic facts and computes the percentage of atomic facts supported by a reliable knowledge source. We conduct an extensive human evaluation to obtain FACTSCOREs of people biographies generated by several state-of-the-art commercial LMs—InstructGPT, ChatGPT, and the retrieval-augmented PerplexityAI—and report new analysis demonstrating the need for such a fine-grained score (e.g., ChatGPT only achieves 58%). Since human evaluation is costly, we also introduce an automated model that estimates FACTSCORE using retrieval and a strong language model, with less than a 2% error rate. Finally, we use this automated metric to evaluate 6,500 generations from a new set of 13 recent LMs that would have cost $26K if evaluated by humans, with various findings: GPT-4 and ChatGPT are more factual than public models, and Vicuna and Alpaca are some of the best public models. FACTSCORE is available for public use via pip install factscore.

### Introduction & Motivation
The growing use of large language models (LMs) for generating long-form text has raised concerns regarding the factual accuracy of their outputs. Evaluating whether the information generated is accurate is compounded by the presence of conflicting facts within said outputs and the high cost and time requirement of human evaluations. This paper showcases FACTSCORE, which quantifies the factual accuracy of LMs by assessing the support for generated atomic facts using established knowledge sources. 

### Methodology
The core technique, FACTSCORE, assesses the factual precision of language model generations by breaking down outputs into atomic facts—short statements that encapsulate individual pieces of information. The process consists of:
1. **Atomic Fact Extraction:** Each generation is decomposed into atomic facts, using guidelines from prior work (Nenkova and Passonneau, 2004) with an average of 4.4 facts per sentence (40% unsupported).
2. **Fact Validation:** Each atomic fact is evaluated against a trusted knowledge source (e.g., Wikipedia). A binary score is assigned to each fact:
   \[
   f(y) = \frac{1}{|Ay|} \sum_{a \in Ay} I[a \text{ is supported by } C]
   \]
   with FACTSCORE defined as:
   \[
   FACTSCORE(M) = E_{x \in X}[f(M_x)]
   \]
3. **Model Architecture:** The architectures of the LMs studied include InstructGPT (text-davinci-003), ChatGPT, and PerplexityAI. Human evaluations determined that InstructGPT achieved a FACTSCORE of 42.5%, ChatGPT at 58.3%, and PerplexityAI at 71.5%.
4. **Training Procedure:** The assessment involved human annotators who worked in pairs to tag atomic facts where they were either Supported, Not-supported, or Irrelevant according to their relevance to the prompt and validation against Wikipedia content. The labor-intensive human evaluation cost approximately $4 per generation.
5. **Automated Estimator:** An automated evaluation model leverages retrieval mechanisms and strong LMs to approximate FACTSCORE with less than 2% error.

- **Datasets:** Models were prompted to generate biographical texts for 183 entities sampled from Wikidata.
- **Metrics Employed:** The accuracy of outputs was evaluated using FACTSCORE.

### Discussion & Conclusion
The primary takeaway from the introduced FACTSCORE methodology is its capability for a fine-grained evaluation of factual precision in long-form text generation, revealing that even state-of-the-art models produced outputs with substantial factual inaccuracies. A notable limitation acknowledged is the focus on factual precision alone without considering recall or other qualitative metrics.
