# 6. Discussion

## Key Findings

**Finding 1: Margin as geometric distance to the alignment decision boundary.** The central empirical result — that pre-alignment confidence margin predicts post-alignment argmax flip with AUROC = 0.867–0.909 for DPO-aligned models — admits a natural geometric interpretation. The pre-alignment logit distribution defines a simplex over answer tokens, and the confidence margin is proportional to the distance from the base model's answer distribution to the nearest boundary where the top-1 and top-2 answers tie. Alignment, as an optimization procedure operating over the same parameter space, perturbs the logit geometry. Items that begin near the decision boundary require only small perturbations to cross it; items far from the boundary are robust to all but the largest perturbations. The margin therefore serves as a proxy for geometric vulnerability — not to alignment in general, but to any perturbation of structured (non-random) form. The non-isotropy finding (RQ2) confirms that alignment perturbations are indeed structured, completing the chain of reasoning: structured perturbations along the dominant perturbation axis will disproportionately flip low-margin items, and this is precisely what we observe. This geometric framing suggests that the margin-based predictor is not an artifact of the specific models studied — it should apply to any alignment method that (a) produces non-isotropic perturbations and (b) does not deliberately target low-margin items for stabilization.

**Finding 2: Non-isotropy reveals that alignment is structured computation, not noise.** The anisotropy ratios of 2.9× (DPO) and 4.6× (SFT) relative to the isotropic control of 1.13 establish that alignment-induced logit perturbations are not well-approximated as random additive noise. This has implications beyond the margin predictor. If alignment were isotropic noise, then the resulting model's error distribution would be essentially unpredictable from pre-alignment signals, and the best a practitioner could do would be to evaluate the aligned model directly. The fact that perturbations are concentrated along dominant eigenvectors means that the alignment transformation has identifiable structure — structure that, in principle, could be characterized from a sample of pre/post alignment logit pairs and used to predict behavior on unseen items. The dominant eigenvector of the perturbation covariance captures the "direction" in which alignment most strongly reshapes the answer probability simplex. Future work analyzing what semantic properties of items project strongly onto this dominant eigenvector could further illuminate what alignment is doing to model representations.

**Finding 3: DPO confidence-dependent amplification as a behavioral signature.** The unexpected finding from RQ3 — that DPO amplifies logit perturbation variance monotonically with pre-alignment confidence (Q5/Q1 ratio = 4.79×) while SFT is flat (Q5/Q1 ratio ≈ 1.26×) — is potentially informative for understanding DPO's dynamics. The pairwise preference objective in DPO does not operate uniformly across items: items on which the base model is already confident are more likely to appear as "chosen" responses in the preference dataset, and DPO's gradient updates on high-likelihood chosen responses can produce large parameter updates when the base model already assigns high mass to that response. SFT's cross-entropy objective does not have this asymmetry; it penalizes incorrect responses proportionally to their probability mass, producing more uniform gradient magnitudes. The behavioral signature we report — DPO reinforces confident decisions aggressively while leaving uncertain decisions relatively undisturbed — could be characterized as a form of confidence compounding, and may have implications for calibration and overconfidence in DPO-aligned models beyond the MCQ setting studied here.

## Limitations

**1. PPO models unavailable.** The original experimental design called for a three-way comparison: DPO, SFT, and PPO. The PPO-aligned model family targeted for this study was unavailable at the time of data collection. The DPO vs. SFT comparison presented here therefore cannot speak to whether the behavioral asymmetry found in RQ3 is a DPO-specific property or a property shared with other RLHF-based methods. This is acceptable because (a) DPO is the dominant current alignment method and the margin-flip result stands independently of the PPO comparison, and (b) the SFT vs. DPO contrast is already scientifically informative.

**2. Primary evidence from one DPO pair.** The strong existence result (β₁ = −4.33, AUROC = 0.867–0.909, η² = 0.289) derives entirely from pair 2 (tulu-2-7b). The SFT pair 4 replicates the directional effect but with AUROC = 0.609, below the discriminative threshold. Two additional model pairs were excluded due to infrastructure failures. This is acceptable because the tulu-2 pair is a well-documented, publicly released model pair with a clean DPO training provenance, and the AUROC values replicate across three independent benchmarks, providing within-pair generalization evidence. Replication across additional DPO model families remains an important next step.

**3. H-M3 and H-M4 not executed.** Two planned mechanistic analyses — an interaction term between margin and alignment method (H-M3) and a cosine projection analysis of the flip direction onto the dominant perturbation eigenvector (H-M4) — were not executed due to scope constraints. These analyses would have further characterized the geometric structure underlying RQ2 and provided a more direct test of the boundary-crossing mechanism. Their absence means the mechanistic argument remains partially inferential. The core existence result (RQ1) does not depend on these analyses.

**4. MCQ-specific results.** All experiments are conducted on multiple-choice question benchmarks with 4-answer options. The logit delta is therefore a 4-dimensional vector, and the margin is defined over 4 choices. Generalization to free-form generation — where the output space is the full vocabulary and the "answer" is a sequence rather than a token — is not tested. The geometric argument in Finding 1 extends in principle to free-form settings (where confidence in the top generated sequence relative to alternatives plays the analogous role), but empirical validation is required.

## Broader Impact

The margin-based predictor described in this paper has a direct positive application as a **pre-deployment auditing tool**: before deploying an aligned model, practitioners can score their evaluation benchmark items by base model confidence margin, identify the low-margin items most vulnerable to alignment-induced answer changes, and subject those items to targeted post-alignment review. This could reduce the rate at which alignment inadvertently flips previously correct answers in safety-critical domains (e.g., medical, legal, scientific MCQ evaluations) without requiring exhaustive re-evaluation of the full item set.

A potential concern is that the same predictor could be used adversarially — for example, to construct evaluation sets that consist entirely of low-margin items, maximizing the chance that alignment degrades performance, or to select alignment training targets that deliberately destabilize low-margin items. We believe the mitigating factor is that the findings are presented under open science principles: making the vulnerability mechanism public allows defenders (alignment researchers, model auditors, benchmark designers) to respond, while the attacker must know the specific model's margin distribution and alignment procedure. We recommend that benchmark designers include a mix of margin quintiles to avoid inadvertently producing low-margin-heavy evaluations.

All code, data, and model checkpoints used in this study are released publicly to enable replication and extension.
