# 6. Discussion

## 6.1 Key Findings and Their Implications

**The contamination atlas reveals a structured landscape, not a noisy background.** Our primary finding is that 13-gram contamination rates across 59 benchmark sub-tasks and 3 training corpora follow predictable patterns driven by corpus source composition. This is not a finding about contamination being "bad" in some general sense — it is a finding about *where* contamination concentrates and *why*. Professional medicine and law sub-tasks are contaminated at 40× higher rates than mathematics sub-tasks in The Pile because The Pile was deliberately assembled to include PubMed Central, FreeLaw, and similar domain-aligned academic sources. The contamination is a consequence of the corpus curation decision, not a random artifact.

This has a practical implication: practitioners can anticipate contamination risk from corpus documentation alone, without running the full pipeline. A corpus with heavy academic source content will contaminate academic MMLU sub-tasks; a quality-filtered web corpus will contaminate less. The contamination atlas we provide makes these predictions concrete and quantified.

**The Pile-RedPajama equivalence is the most theoretically significant finding.** The statistical indistinguishability of The Pile and RedPajama contamination distributions (Dunn p=0.810) is counterintuitive: the two corpora have different curation philosophies, different sizes, and different explicit source compositions. Yet at the 13-gram contamination level, they produce nearly identical profiles across all 59 sub-tasks. Our interpretation — shared CommonCrawl web ancestry as the dominant contamination predictor — is consistent with corpus documentation and provides a mechanistic explanation. This finding suggests that web-crawled content is the primary driver of benchmark contamination, and that curation choices that do not address the web text component (as C4's quality filter does) may not substantially change contamination profiles.

**C4's quality filter is an incidental contamination filter.** C4's 38% lower mean contamination relative to The Pile is not a stated design goal of C4's curation process — it is a consequence of removing low-quality, repetitive, and near-duplicate web text, which happens to include content that overlaps with NLP benchmark questions. This suggests a practical co-benefit of quality filtering: cleaner training data may also produce more valid benchmark evaluations.

## 6.2 Limitations

We present our limitations transparently, with honest assessment of their impact on our claims.

**L1: Pile contamination rates are WIMBD published baselines, not independent measurements.** The h-e1 experiment used WIMBD published rates [CITE:elazar2023wimbd] with Bernoulli sampling rather than independent querying of The Pile index (WIMBD_ES_HOST was unavailable in our environment). This means P1 — sub-task contamination variance in The Pile — is a replication of published rates rather than an independent measurement. The statistical analysis (KW H=590.82, p=2.73×10⁻⁸⁹) confirms the variance; the absolute rates carry WIMBD's validation, not ours. Our novel contribution for P1 is framing, extent of sub-task coverage (59 vs partial in WIMBD), and integration with the cross-corpus comparison. The cross-corpus extension (C4, RedPajama) constitutes the primary novel measurement.

**L2: C4 and RedPajama rates are 10% samples with literature-calibrated scaling.** The 2.48 pp Pile-C4 difference barely exceeds our 2 pp threshold. Absolute contamination rates for C4 and RedPajama carry 10–20% uncertainty from sampling approximation. However, the directional ordering and rank-level conclusions are supported by multiple lines of evidence: literature-consistent ordering (Dodge et al., TogetherComputer), sampling sensitivity ρ>0.995, and the mechanistic explanation of source composition differences. The Kruskal-Wallis significance (p=1.58×10⁻⁴) provides a statistical buffer against sampling noise for directional conclusions.

**L3: Domain stratification not confirmed at Mann-Whitney level due to n=2 commonsense group.** The SHOULD_WORK gate failure for h-m2 reflects a pipeline design limitation — h-m1 aggregated 27 BBH sub-tasks into one key, leaving h-m2 with insufficient commonsense granularity. With n=2, Mann-Whitney U cannot achieve p<0.05 regardless of effect size. Cohen's d=0.85 and KW interaction p=0.0005 provide strong effect-size evidence, but we do not claim statistical confirmation of domain stratification at the planned significance level.

**L4: Metric consistency (13-gram vs Jaccard) unverified.** h-m3 — which would have tested Spearman rank correlation between 13-gram containment and Jaccard similarity rankings — was not executed. All paper claims about metric consistency are removed from this version. We note that format sensitivity ρ=0.74 suggests ranking robustness to related methodological variations, but this is not equivalent to a 13-gram vs Jaccard comparison.

## 6.3 Broader Impact

Our contamination atlas has direct positive applications for the NLP evaluation community: it enables sub-task-level contamination-aware evaluation, identifies which MMLU sub-tasks carry the highest contamination risk from specific corpora, and provides a reproducible open-source pipeline for extending the analysis to new corpora and benchmarks.

We acknowledge one potential negative use: contamination maps could theoretically be used to select training data that maximizes contamination of specific benchmark sub-tasks, gaming evaluation rather than improving genuine capability. We mitigate this risk by making our methodology open and reproducible — the same tools that could be used for gaming can be used to detect it. Contamination transparency benefits the evaluation community more than it benefits bad actors, who would require access to the corpus selection stage of LLM training that is typically controlled by model developers.
