# Related Work

Our work sits at the intersection of three active research areas: entropy-based uncertainty quantification for LLMs, consistency-based hallucination detection, and hallucination benchmarking. We organize related work to show how each stream contributes to the open problem of controlled cross-signal comparison, and why existing work leaves that problem unresolved.

## Entropy-Based Uncertainty Quantification

Token-level entropy — the mean Shannon entropy over per-token output distributions — is among the simplest UQ signals derivable from an LLM's generation process without additional inference cost [Kadavath et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2022]. Kadavath et al. [2022] demonstrate that larger Anthropic models exhibit reasonable self-calibration (P(True) signal), but this analysis targets proprietary models and does not systematically measure hallucination discrimination ability via AUROC on standard factual QA benchmarks.

**Semantic entropy** [Kuhn et al., 2023] advances beyond token-level entropy by aggregating uncertainty over semantic equivalence classes rather than token sequences. The key step — NLI-based clustering of N stochastic responses — filters surface-form variation, in theory producing a cleaner uncertainty signal. Kuhn et al. (2023) validate semantic entropy on TriviaQA and Natural Questions with several LLMs, reporting AUROC ≈ 0.78 — a substantial improvement over token entropy baselines. The lorenzkuhn/semantic_uncertainty repository provides an official implementation using deberta-large-mnli for NLI clustering.

Two important properties of this prior evaluation are worth noting: (1) TriviaQA and NQ responses are typically longer and more syntactically varied than short factual QA responses; (2) the NLI aggregation rate (the fraction of examples for which semantic clustering successfully groups responses) is not reported in the original paper. Our work shows that these properties are critical for predicting whether semantic entropy will be non-degenerate on a new benchmark.

Xiao et al. [2022] provide a broader comparison of UQ approaches (MC dropout, deep ensembles, temperature scaling) on NLU tasks, demonstrating that the choice of UQ method matters — but they focus on classification tasks rather than generative hallucination detection. Our work focuses specifically on the open-ended QA generation setting that characterizes HaluEval-QA.

## Consistency-Based Hallucination Detection

**SelfCheckGPT** [Manakul et al., 2023] takes an orthogonal approach to entropy-based methods: rather than measuring uncertainty from internal distributions, it measures the consistency of N stochastic generations against a greedy response using BERTScore, NLI, or n-gram overlap. The key assumption is that *consistent* responses indicate confidence (and likely factual accuracy), while *inconsistent* responses signal hallucination.

Manakul et al. (2023) validate SelfCheckGPT on WikiBio biography generation, reporting AUC-PR ≈ 0.85 for the BERTScore variant with N=5 samples. This is a strong result — but WikiBio involves long-form biographical text where factual confidence naturally correlates with consistent generation. HaluEval-QA's hallucinations are constructed differently: they are ChatGPT-generated confident-sounding incorrect answers [Li et al., 2023]. When hallucinations are *designed* to be internally consistent, the SelfCheckGPT assumption inverts: consistent generation may indicate a confidently stated wrong answer rather than a factually correct one. Our below-random AUROC result (0.3562) is consistent with this inversion.

Confidence elicitation via verbalized uncertainty [Xiong et al., 2023] studies how well LLMs can express their own uncertainty in natural language, finding significant calibration gaps across LLM families. While complementary to our work, verbalized confidence requires prompting the model to express uncertainty and does not rely on internal probability signals — a different deployment paradigm. We focus exclusively on post-hoc UQ signals derivable from a single standard inference call.

## Hallucination Benchmarks

**HaluEval** [Li et al., 2023] is a large-scale hallucination evaluation benchmark covering QA, dialogue, and summarization tasks. The QA subset provides binary hallucination labels generated by ChatGPT: for each original question-answer pair, ChatGPT was prompted to produce a plausible but incorrect alternative answer. Li et al. (2023) note that the QA subset is the most reliable of the three HaluEval tasks. Critically, the ChatGPT-generated hallucinations are designed to be confident-sounding and internally coherent — a property that, as our results suggest, may adversely interact with consistency-based UQ methods.

Prior work using HaluEval has focused on training models to classify hallucinations [Li et al., 2023] or measuring the hallucination rate of LLMs [Xiong et al., 2023], rather than evaluating how well UQ signals from the LLM itself predict the binary labels. We fill this gap.

Conformal Language Modeling [Quach et al., 2023] frames hallucination mitigation as an abstention problem with distribution-free coverage guarantees. This approach requires calibration data and targets the abstention setting rather than per-example hallucination detection — a distinct problem formulation from our comparative AUROC evaluation.

## The Cross-Signal Comparison Gap

The fundamental limitation of the existing literature is methodological: each method paper validates on its own benchmark with its own model under conditions optimized for that method. Semantic entropy on TriviaQA/NQ [Kuhn et al., 2023], SelfCheckGPT on WikiBio [Manakul et al., 2023], P(True) on proprietary models [Kadavath et al., 2022]. This siloed evaluation means that a practitioner who wants to know "which UQ method should I use for short factual QA on an open-source LLM?" has no direct evidence to draw on.

Xiong et al. [2023] compare confidence elicitation methods across LLMs, but do not focus on entropy-based UQ or HaluEval-QA, and do not control for model, dataset, and compute budget simultaneously. To our knowledge, our work is the first to apply all three major UQ signal families — token entropy, NLI-based semantic entropy, and sampling consistency — to the same benchmark (HaluEval-QA) with the same LLM (LLaMA-2-7B-chat) under matched conditions (same 2,000-example stratified sample, same N=5 stochastic budget, same bootstrap CI evaluation protocol).

The result of this controlled comparison is sobering: none of the three methods discriminate hallucinations reliably on HaluEval-QA under standard configurations. But the controlled design is precisely what allows us to attribute this failure to specific mechanism violations rather than evaluation confounds — a stronger scientific contribution than either a positive result from an uncontrolled comparison or a generic null result without diagnosis.
