# Abstract

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) has emerged as a compelling method for training code-generating language models from execution feedback, yet its effectiveness depends on a structural precondition that practitioners often overlook: at least one completion in each training group must receive non-zero reward for advantage normalization to carry gradient signal. We show that this precondition is systematically violated when 7B-class models are trained on competitive programming problems with binary execution reward — a regime where near-zero solve rates render virtually every training group degenerate. In a controlled comparison of function-level (APPS+CodeContests) and repository-level (SWE-bench Verified) GRPO training under identical configuration, we find a **76× advantage variance gap** (0.004 vs. 0.317, $p = 5.34 \times 10^{-44}$, Cohen's $d = 1.904$), driven by the difference between ≈0% and ≈6% positive reward rates. We further validate the training infrastructure for a difficulty-stratified curriculum GRPO experiment (4 conditions: curriculum, uniform, easy-only, hard-only) and identify that even easy-tier competitive programming problems yield zero reward density for base models at initialization — a finding that sharpens the conditions under which curriculum ordering can rescue degenerate training. Our results establish advantage variance as a principled early diagnostic for execution-feedback RL and provide mechanistic grounding for curriculum approaches that target problems within the model's current solvability range.
